While it's true that there has been a significant shift towards microservices in the software architecture space, it doesn't necessarily mean that Torvalds' stance on monolithic kernels for operating systems was wrong. It's important to note that microservices and microkernels serve different purposes and are used in different contexts. Microservices are an architectural style that structures an application as a collection of loosely coupled services, which implement business capabilities. This architecture style has gained popularity due to its scalability and the speed that changes can be made. It allows for the independent deployment of different parts of a system, something that's highly valued in today's rapid development cycles. Microkernels, on the other hand, pertain to the structure of an operating system. A microkernel aims to minimize the amount of code running in privileged mode, thereby reducing the OS's complexity and improving its security and reliability. Torvalds' stance, as exemplified in the development of Linux, was in favor of monolithic kernels, where the entire operating system works in the kernel space. This allows for high efficiency and performance since inter-process communication can be faster than in a microkernel design. However, the choice between a monolithic kernel and a microkernel often depends on the specific requirements and constraints of a project. There is no absolute right or wrong, only trade-offs. In terms of academic references, a paper titled "Microkernels meet Recursive Virtual Machines" by Bershad et al. (1995) discusses some of the efficiencies of monolithic designs over microkernels. On the other hand, a paper titled "Exokernel: An Operating System Architecture for Application-Level Resource Management" by Engler et al. (1995) presents the idea of minimizing the kernel for efficiency and flexibility, a concept similar to microkernels. To summarize, while microservices and microkernels might be seen as the future in some contexts due to their flexibility and modularity, it doesn't negate the validity or efficiency of monolithic kernels in other contexts, thus Torvalds' stance is not necessarily proven wrong.
